Target Field

Mobile Application UX & UI Design for Concessions Sales

Check It Out

Are Stadium Owners Leaving Money on the Table?


Sports Business journal estimates that revenue from in-stadium concession sales in American sporting facilities breaches $10 Billion annually. Even minor percentage increases in fan spending would translate into huge gains for owners. In this project, I set out to create a prototype of a mobile application that would improve overall guest experience and increase sales at stadium events. I believe that a well-designed prototype that solves guest inconveniences would serve as a proof of concept for an application that could be developed and scaled to stadiums nationwide.

Launch the Prototype


Driving Business Goals By Solving Guest Pain Points

In order to create a lift in concessions sales, my application will provide stadiums with the technology to drive their business goals by better serving their guests. In order to create a better guest experience, I seek to solve two key pain points for stadium goers:

  1. Not wanting to leave their seats to stand in long lines for concessions
  2. Not having a cashless point of sale system for vendors walking through the stands

Existing research supports the idea that solving these two user challenges would create an increase in sales. According to a survey done by Saint John Fisher College, “87% of [respondents] would use an in-seat ordering system more frequently as well as order more food if it indeed allowed them to not miss game action and avoid long wait lines.” I believe that a well-designed prototype that solves these inconveniences would serve as a proof of concept for an application that could be developed and scaled to stadiums nationwide.

Background Research


User Interviews

I began the background research for my application by briefly interviewing potential users on their experience with ordering food outside of a stadium and what they would need from an application to make ordering within a stadium easier. I spoke with six users with varying demographic backgrounds and levels of stadium-going experience. My interviews centered around the following questions:

  1. What would you need from an application in order that would help you order food while in a stadium?
  2. What experience do you have with ordering food delivery? Who in your opinion does it best?
  3. Would you be more likely to order food or drinks if you could do it from your phone? Would you be willing to pay an additional fee for this service?

Competetive Analysis

To help me empathize with how users can best understand my application, I looked at the technologies they would be familiar with. By outlining the features that users have come to expect as table stakes for functional payment tools and food ordering, I can better define the product requirements for the application. I first began by looking at technologies that directly overlap with the goals of the application, and then spread my research out to the secondary and tertiary levels of technologies that offer similar functionality but in different contexts.

Paper Prototype Testing

I then printed out my screens from Balsamiq and used hand drawn elements to simulate my users' journey through my flows. I tested my product on three users, asking them for feedback while having them complete several tasks. I focused my research on high level questions of the users' understanding of my product. The paper prototype phase helped me quickly and cheaply receive the feedback I would need to create a clickable prototype. From my lowidelity testing, I was able to design an additional feature, identify a need for more instructions, and refine my screen layout to better suit user needs. Redesigns from Paper Prototype Testing

Pick Up and Delivery:

Many of my users wanted to know if they could place orders for pick up. I had initially intended to build this into a later step of the order checkout process. My redesign includes this step directly in the user's cart and provides the location for delivery or pickup to the user before they place their order. Depending on what option they choose, they are then presented with separate confirmation screens for either pickup or delivery.

Prompting Outside of App Interactions:

Several users struggled with making cashless payments to vendors. In order for a charge to be sent to the application, the user is required to ask the vendor to charge it to their section and seat number. Requiring them to perform this real world action presented a unique challenge for designing an intuitive user experience. To help them understand the application, I created an instructional onboarding flow for new users. The flow also doubles as a set up process for payment and seating information. During the onboarding sequence, users are walked through the two flows with easy to understand, step by step instructions.

Screen Reorganization:

Through my testing, I found that the bottom menu was not functioning as I desired. Users expected the credit card icon to take them to setting up their payment information. To solve this issue, I created a drawer and hamburger navigation that presents both text and icons. My users also stated that they would expect to be able to search for items, which I also added to the homescreen.

Clickable Prototype Design from Paper Prototype Testing

Using the feedback from my paper prototype testing, I created an interactive redesign of my product in Balsamiq. This build out my product design added pickup orders, payment setup, order cancelling, and easter eggs to help me test vendor payments by simulating the user's order being placed. When testing my prototype, I asked users to place an order of a bratwurst from a specific vendor for pickup and then ran them through making cashless payments. This version remained low fidelity and allowed me to run a longer, more in depth testing of my application design with users navigating the journey on a mobile device.

High Fidelity Design


I used the findings from my clickable prototype tests to guide my high fidelity design. Using Sketch and Figma, I was able to increase the level of fidelity which afforded me the use of more complex interactions and helped to simplify my product. Along with these redesigns, I created a style guide and a set of user interface patterns that helped create an overall more consistent and intuitive product.
